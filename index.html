The Journey East

How a kid from communist East Germany ended up founding a strategy consultancy in Shanghai — and why his parents weren't thrilled about it.

Falk grew up in Dresden in the GDR — behind the Iron Curtain, in a place so isolated they couldn't even receive Western television. After the wall came down, he carved a career through TBWA Berlin, DDB Düsseldorf, and eventually a fateful meeting on a balcony in Singapore that rerouted him through London and ultimately to Shanghai, where he's been for over a decade. His consultancy, Hui Lui, literally means "come back strategies" — but poetically, it means finding a different way to your goal.

When I told my parents I was moving to China, they were not very happy. I grew up in a communist regime — and now you move into a communist country again? That's weird.

— Falk Fuhrmann
背景 Context

What is Hui Lui?

A "fluid brand innovation consultancy" — not an agency. Just Falk and his Chinese partner, building bespoke teams around projects for multinationals entering or operating in China.

Chapter 02 · 差别
10:58 – 13:13

China Is Not Asia

Why companies that treat China as just another Asian market get it fundamentally wrong.

China operates as a completely separate business region — different media ecosystem, different campaign development, different consumer behavior. You have to physically be there to see behind the curated surface. At the same time, China is deeply ethnocentric, making it hard for Chinese teams to develop for other Asian markets. It cuts both ways.

You have to be in China to understand how things are here. You have to live here and see behind the surface — things you don't see when you come to a curated market visit.

— Falk Fuhrmann
翻转 Perspective Flip

Ethnocentrism is a two-way street

China, like America, is deeply ethnocentric. Chinese teams struggle to develop for Asia just as much as Western teams struggle to develop for China. The blindness runs in both directions.

深入 Go Deeper

China's separate digital universe

WeChat instead of WhatsApp, Weibo instead of Twitter, Douyin instead of TikTok (yes, they're different products), Baidu instead of Google. These platforms have evolved far beyond their Western analogues.

思考 Reflect

背景 Why it matters for brands

Separate ecosystem = separate strategy

Different media platforms, different payment systems, different consumer psychology. A campaign that works in Seoul or Tokyo won't work in Shanghai. China requires its own team, its own budget, its own creative — not an adaptation of your global work.

Chapter 03 · 区域
13:13 – 18:53

The China Within China

Living in Shanghai is like living in New York — you have no clue about the rest of the country.

Falk experiences multiple Chinas — his office in Shanghai, teaching in Hangzhou, family on Hainan island. Even global brands that claim national distribution often have "pockets of distribution." His business partner from Sichuan sees other provinces completely differently than someone from the northeast. Think of it like Europe, not a single country.

Living in Shanghai is like living in New York. You have no clue about the rest of China.

— Falk Fuhrmann
背景 Context

China's scale is hard to grasp

1.4 billion people across 23 provinces and 5 autonomous regions. Five time zones (officially one). Mutually unintelligible dialects. A marketer treating this as one market is like treating Portugal and Finland as the same country.

Regional languages, cuisines, and identities

Falk compares China to Europe. Here's why.

🌶
Sichuan · 四川
Language: Sichuanese (largely unintelligible to Mandarin speakers). Cuisine: Fiery, numbing málà hotpot, kung pao chicken, mapo tofu. Falk's business partner is from here — she sees every other province differently.
🥟
Shanghai · 上海
Language: Shanghainese (Wu dialect family). Cuisine: Sweet, refined — xiaolongbao, hairy crab, red-braised pork. Where Falk lives. "Like New York — you have no clue about the rest of China."
🍜
Guangdong · 广东
Language: Cantonese (entirely different tonal system, 9 tones vs Mandarin's 4). Cuisine: Dim sum, char siu, wonton noodles, roast goose. Hong Kong's cultural motherland.
🐑
Dongbei · 东北
Language: Northeastern Mandarin (the "Texas drawl" of China). Cuisine: Heavy, hearty — lamb stew, pickled cabbage, dumplings by the hundred. Falk notes people here see the world completely differently.
🏖
Hainan · 海南
Language: Hainanese (Min dialect family). Cuisine: Coconut chicken, Wenchang chicken, tropical seafood. Where Falk's family lives. "Used to be the least developed province." Now home to the surfer generation.
🍵
Zhejiang · 浙江
Language: Wu dialects (related to Shanghainese). Cuisine: Light, fresh — Dongpo pork, West Lake fish. Home to Hangzhou where Falk teaches, and Yiwu, the world's small-goods capital.
🌿
Yunnan · 云南
Language: 25 ethnic minority languages alongside Mandarin. Cuisine: Crossing-the-bridge noodles, wild mushroom hotpot, Pu'er tea. More ethnic diversity than most European countries combined.
🫓
Shaanxi · 陕西
Language: Shaanxi Mandarin (ancient Chang'an accent). Cuisine: Wheat-based — biangbiang noodles, roujiamo (Chinese "burger"), lamb paomo. Xi'an was the original capital for 13 dynasties.

Chapter 04 · 体制
18:53 – 29:10

The Operating System

Not top-down command, but competing mayors running experiments — like a national test laboratory.

The central government sets broad directions; provinces and mayors interpret them differently, compete, and eventually the government picks what works. This explains why rules change province to province, why cities specialize, and why AI adoption is driven by district-level grants.

The US-China trade war: where things stand (April 2026)

This episode dropped during the most volatile moment in US-China trade since 2018. Here's the timeline:

What Falk's perspective adds: While trade policy dominates headlines, Falk shows that the deeper shifts — consumer psychology, brand loyalty flipping, demographic decline, the convenience ecosystem — will matter more long-term than any tariff rate. The system he describes adapts faster than policy can contain it.

翻转 Perspective Flip

Less centralized than you think

From inside, China looks like 30+ provinces running competing experiments — with the central government acting more as referee than dictator.

体制 When the system overbuilds — and when it can't
🏙 Ghost Cities
The mayor economy's most spectacular failure. When competing provinces all bet on urbanization, you get entire cities built for millions — with nobody living in them. Ordos in Inner Mongolia was built for 1 million; fewer than 100,000 came. Chenggong near Kunming, Tianducheng near Hangzhou (complete with a replica Eiffel Tower). At peak, China had an estimated 65 million empty apartments. This is what happens when mayors compete to build without coordinating demand. Some are slowly filling up. Many never will.
🏠 Nail Houses · 钉子户
Ed mentioned them in the podcast: homes that refuse to be demolished, so entire developments are built around them. The Chinese call them "nail houses" — 钉子户 — because they stick up like a nail that won't be hammered down. The most famous: a house in Chongqing that sat alone on a pillar of earth while a construction pit was dug around it for three years. The owners eventually settled — but the image became a symbol of individual resistance in a system built for speed. Legally, China's property rights have strengthened since 2007, but the tension between rapid development and individual holdouts remains one of the system's most visible contradictions.

The Brand Revolution

Western brands went from aspiration to afterthought. The coin has flipped.

Falk drives a Beijing Jeep whose previous owner stuck a Jeep logo on the front. That was the aspiration. Falk took the logo off. That shift is the story of the decade. Pampers went from 50% market share to a fraction. BYD and Xiaomi are serving fundamentally different needs. The uncomfortable truth: Chinese companies simply understand their consumers better.

It's not only about Chinese pride. Chinese brands have developed an advantage of understanding their consumers. They understand our needs much better than those Western brands do.

Huaqiangbei, Shenzhen: the Silicon Valley of Hardware

Imagine walking into a building and assembling a smartphone from scratch using off-the-shelf parts. That's Huaqiangbei — a 1.45 km² district in Shenzhen with 20,000+ vendors, processing ¥400 billion in annual transactions. This is why Falk says "whatever you have in mind, you can build in China."

Concept to prototype
How it works in practice
You arrive with a sketch on your phone. Floor 1: you buy the circuit boards. Floor 3: the screen. Floor 5: the casing. Floor 7: the battery. By afternoon, a prototyping shop across the street assembles it. By evening, you have a working device. Multiple Kickstarter campaigns and Silicon Valley hardware startups have sourced and prototyped here. The key buildings — SEG Electronics Market, Huaqiang Electronics World, Yuanwang Digital Mall — form an ecosystem where the entire global supply chain is compressed into a few city blocks.

Chapter 06 · 便利

The Convenience Culture

Why convenience beats privacy every time in China's digital ecosystem.

Chinese culture is extremely convenience-driven. People trade data privacy for seamlessness. Without WeChat, you can't book a hospital, pay at a grocery, or even pay at a rural vegetable market. The platform evolved through continuous layering until a messaging app became infrastructure.

Without WeChat, I couldn't get an appointment at the hospital, I couldn't pay at the grocery. Even at the vegetable market in rural China, you pay with it. Chinese culture is extremely convenience-driven.

— Falk Fuhrmann
便利 The convenience machine

Chinese apps that have no Western equivalent

Each of these started by solving one problem, then layered until they became infrastructure.

💬
WeChat
Messaging + payments + hospital bookings + government services + mini-programs + social media + utility bills + ride hailing. 1.3 billion users. You literally cannot function in China without it.
🛵
Meituan
Food delivery in 20 min + grocery delivery + hotel booking + movie tickets + bike rental + pharmacy delivery + group deals. 700M+ users. Processes 60M+ orders per day.
🚕
Didi
Ride hailing + shared bikes + car rental + freight transport + autonomous taxis in Wuhan. 600M+ users. Uber left China because they couldn't compete.
🍜
Ele.me
Alibaba's food delivery platform. Competes directly with Meituan. Also delivers groceries, flowers, medicine, pet supplies — anything within 30 minutes.
🛒
Pinduoduo
Group-buying e-commerce. Get lower prices by buying with friends. Gamified shopping. 900M+ users. Now global via Temu. Overtook Alibaba's market cap in 2023.
📕
Xiaohongshu
"Little Red Book." Instagram + Pinterest + product reviews + in-app shopping. 300M+ users. Where Chinese consumers research everything before buying.

In Germany, data privacy is a fundamental right. In China, convenience is a cultural value worth trading for. Neither is inherently right. Which trade-offs do you make without noticing?

Chapter 07 · 失望
41:30 – 58:09

Losing the Future

The biggest change isn't economic — it's psychological. A generation is starting to doubt that tomorrow will be better.

The emotional heart of the conversation. The biggest challenge is the loss of optimism. For decades, salaries rose 20% a year. After COVID, that changed. On Hainan, young graduates become "surfer boys and girls." Women are more educated but face enormous social pressure. The collectivist society is making room for choices it never anticipated.

The biggest challenge for people in China is that they are losing this optimism you always find in developing economies. They have to change how they consume, how they look for meaning in life.

— Falk Fuhrmann

Key Concepts from this Conversation

🏛 Mayor Economy

How governance actually works

Central government sets direction; provincial leaders interpret differently, compete. The government picks winners. Explains industry clusters and regulatory variation.

🔄 The Pendulum

Swings between welcoming and resisting foreign influence

A recurring cycle affecting hiring, consumer preferences, and business strategy.

📱 Layered Innovation

Imitate, then improve relentlessly

Not "move fast and break things" — study, copy, then build layer upon layer. WeChat is the prime example.

⚡ Convenience as Culture

Why consumers trade privacy for seamlessness

A genuine cultural priority. It's why super-apps and frictionless payment became universal in China.

📉 The Optimism Deficit

When a nation stops expecting better

Post-COVID, the assumption that next year will be better has fractured. The psychological shift may matter more than any economic indicator.

🏭 The OEM Machine

Build anything you imagine

China's manufacturing ecosystem lets anyone build anything. This enables the fearlessness and speed of Chinese entrepreneurship.
